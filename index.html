üîê 1. Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Everywhere

Using a strong password is no longer enough. Cyber attackers often gain access to accounts through data leaks or phishing attacks that expose passwords. This is where multi-factor authentication (MFA) becomes critical. MFA adds an additional layer of security by requiring a second form of verification, such as:

  • A one-time code sent via SMS or email
  • A fingerprint or facial recognition scan
  • An authenticator app like Google Authenticator or Authy

Even if your password is compromised, hackers would still need that second factor to gain access.

Pro Tip: Prioritize MFA for your email, banking apps, social media, and cloud storage accounts. These are often the first targets for cybercriminals.

Reusing passwords across multiple sites is a major risk. If one account is compromised, attackers can gain access to others through credential stuffing attacks. A password manager solves this by generating and storing unique, complex passwords for every account.

Reliable password managers like Bitwarden, Dashlane, and 1Password encrypt your passwords and make them accessible only to you.

Pro Tip: Use a strong, memorable passphrase for your master password. Avoid writing passwords down or saving them in plain text on your device. Enable MFA on your password manager for added security.

Your home Wi-Fi and public networks can be entry points for attackers if not properly secured. At home, many people still use default router settings, which are often easy to guess or publicly listed online. In public, connecting to open Wi-Fi without protection can expose your data to interception (man-in-the-middle attacks).

Pro Tip: Change the default username and password of your home router. Use WPA3 encryption (or at least WPA2) on your Wi-Fi. Hide your network SSID (name) if possible. On public Wi-Fi, use a VPN to encrypt your traffic and prevent spying.

Cyberattacks like ransomware can encrypt your files and demand money for their release. Regular backups ensure that even if your data is lost, stolen, or corrupted, you can recover it without paying hackers.

Pro Tip: Use a combination of cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive) and external hard drives. Schedule automatic backups weekly or monthly. Encrypt sensitive data before backing it up.
